## 3. Not One Conversation but Three: Capacity, Emotion, Redesign

The workforce conversation about AI is not one conversation — it's really three. It helps to frame it that way, because part of what makes it difficult is that you may be having one conversation while the person you're speaking with is having another one with you.

The first — the executive conversation — is **capacity reallocation**. Is AI helping us do more of what matters, or is it just making us busier? I speak with a lot of organizations, and the sentiment is: our people feel so busy, but we can't tell what they're busy with. Why is personal productivity increasing while everyone feels so busy? Yes, AI is creating some capacity — but what are we doing with it? Can we convert it into something different?

The second is **emotional adaptation**, and it's real. This chaos creates a roller coaster for everyone, from the ground floor to the penthouse — including the executives personally. The question: will AI be the force that fractures the organization, or the force that mobilizes it?

The third is **work redesign**: can we create new value that was not there before? Will AI allow us to do something we could not do before — become something we could not be before?

These three are very interdependent. Look at the gray boxes underneath: they all depend on each other. If your people are not emotionally engaged, they will not create capacity for you. If you can't create capacity, you can't do the work redesign. That interdependence adds to the complexity of the workforce conversation.

Everyone's first instinct is to chase capacity as the first measurable win, because it's easy to say "we put AI here, we save minutes and hours on this job." It's a check-the-box. But your first visible win should be with your people: bring them along on the journey so they feel ownership of what's going to happen and are part of the experimentation and innovation — so that instead of worrying about their jobs, they are excited to have the jobs of tomorrow and do the kinds of things they could not do before. Once you convert those first two into work redesign — intentional job redesign — that's the most durable way to think about all of this. All three are important. You will feel pressure at different points in your workforce-planning journey, but you want to be intentional about what you're trying to win at which point, and what you'll do with it.

So we created a shared language around **capability**. This matters because it sets the stage for how you'll look at a particular job.

- **Core capabilities** are the skills and tasks always owned by this job, no matter what — whether you have 3, 10, or 15 years of experience, wherever you sit, whatever the organization. If you are a software engineer, you own these skills at most organizations; if you are a data scientist, this is always part of your job.
- **Contextual capabilities** depend on conditions: how your team is set up, how much trust you personally have, how senior you are, the business context. Maybe you own them, maybe you don't.
- **Shared capabilities**: regardless of our jobs, all of us contribute to a set of shared capabilities for the organization. They differ by job, but for any job we analyze, we look at how it contributes to shared capabilities and shared outcomes.

## 10. Work Redesign: The Third Battle

This brings us to redesigning the workforce — the third battle. Once AI frees capacity, we need to define what humans do next. And it cannot be "let's address the backlog that's been sitting there for the past three years." It should be something new, inspiring, value-creating for the business — that's where we need to fully move in (?).

Break it down analytically:

- **Routine** — AI will automate it, or we should leverage AI to automate it.
- **Coordination** — handoffs between the various systems; AI can definitely help accelerate it.
- **Sense-making** — analyzing and looking for opportunities from an innovation perspective; AI can help bolster humans, taking an idea and formulating it further.
- **Connections** — building relationships, deeper understanding of the business and the industry; that, AI will multiply.

Human work falls into these categories, and AI automates the routine, enhances coordination, and supports sense-making. This framework can really help redesign the future of your workforce (?).

From the data scientist's perspective, here's what it looks like. **AI-assisted development becomes the norm**: AI-driven tools automate data exploration, identifying feature variables, and feature engineering — and in most cases, with AutoML tools, model selection: I don't even need to know which model is best; the system identifies it. **Ownership boundaries blur**: data scientists are increasingly measured by business outcomes more than by the models they create. It's not just model accuracy but impact. I've been in discussions where a data scientist defends "my model's providing 98% accuracy," while the business says "but that hasn't helped customer attrition, or moved the needle by even the 2% or 5% we expected." You can have one of the best models with the highest accuracy, but if it doesn't move the dial on customer retention or growth, it doesn't have business impact. The role also **shifts toward product-centric outcomes**: collaborating with partners (?) and different business units, building resilient platforms that various lines of business can leverage — and more importantly, the ability to prototype quickly, iterate, and ship. I've seen organizations where data scientists prototype something over a weekend and have a model ready by Monday — and then it takes about three months to get that model into production and integrated into the workflow. That's where these roles and the collaboration become important.

**Kabeh Vaziri:** We are definitely exploring. I can tell you there is definitely [not] one final answer here. I think junior talent will be like that: hiring for the ability to learn and unlearn, the ability to reinvent yourself, and of course some core technical capabilities — but without double-clicking too hard on the specific technical capabilities, because there is a very good chance those skills will be somewhat outdated by some future.
